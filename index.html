Three techniques — click each to expand
CoT
Chain of Thought
Make the model show its reasoning
For complex problems — break it down before answering
▶
Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting asks the model to work through a problem step by step before producing a final answer. Instead of jumping to a conclusion, the model is instructed to reason out loud — making each step visible and auditable. This mirrors how a good clinician thinks through a case, or how a researcher builds an argument. The reasoning chain is often more useful than the final answer, because it lets you spot where the logic goes wrong.
How to use it
1
Give full context — the more specific your setup, the more useful the reasoning chain
2
Add the trigger phrase — "think through this step by step," "reason through each consideration," or "walk me through your reasoning before answering"
3
Read the reasoning, not just the conclusion — this is where errors surface. A plausible-sounding answer can rest on flawed logic
Examples
Prompt
A 24-year-old woman presents to the ED with 3 days of fever, right flank pain, and dysuria. Her UA shows pyuria and bacteriuria. She is 10 weeks pregnant. Think through step by step: what is the most likely diagnosis, what are the treatment considerations specific to pregnancy, and what complications should be monitored for?
Prompt
I am designing a retrospective cohort study to examine whether early corticosteroid use in hospitalized COVID-19 patients reduces 30-day mortality. Reason through step by step: what are the key confounders I need to control for, what inclusion and exclusion criteria would strengthen validity, and what are the main threats to internal validity in this design?
Prompt
I am studying the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system for my internal medicine shelf. Walk me through step by step how ACE inhibitors lower blood pressure, why they cause hyperkalemia, and why they are contraindicated in bilateral renal artery stenosis — connecting each mechanism to the physiology.
Use when
Clinical reasoningWorking through a differential, understanding why a treatment is chosen
Study designThinking through methodology, confounders, validity threats
Mechanism questionsUnderstanding pathophysiology, pharmacology, or biochemistry chains
Case presentationsStructuring your reasoning before writing your H&P assessment
Important: The reasoning chain makes errors visible — but only if you know enough to spot them. CoT reduces error rates; it doesn't eliminate them. Your own knowledge is still the verification layer.
Sahoo et al. (2024), arXiv:2402.07927
CoVe
Chain of Verification
Make the model check its own facts
For fact-sensitive outputs — structured self-audit to catch hallucinations
▶
Chain of Verification (CoVe) is a four-step process where the model generates an initial response, identifies specific factual claims it made, answers verification questions about each claim independently (without anchoring to the original response), then produces a corrected final answer. The critical insight: verification questions are answered in isolation, so the model cannot simply echo what it already said. Research shows CoVe measurably reduces hallucination while preserving accuracy.
The four steps
1
Generate — model produces an initial response
2
Plan verification — model identifies specific factual claims that could be wrong
3
Answer independently — each claim is verified without reference to the original response
4
Revise — final answer is updated based on what the verification found
Examples
Turn 1
What are the diagnostic criteria for systemic lupus erythematosus and what laboratory findings would support the diagnosis?
Turn 2 — verification
Now list each specific factual claim you just made — criteria, thresholds, antibody sensitivities. Verify each one independently, without referring to your previous answer, and correct any errors in a revised response.
Turn 1
Summarize the key findings of the RECOVER trial on long COVID symptoms, including the primary endpoint, population, and main results.
Turn 2 — verification
Identify every specific claim you made — trial name, endpoints, sample sizes, percentages, conclusions. Verify each independently and revise your summary to correct any inaccuracies. Flag any claims you cannot verify with confidence.
Turn 1
What are the Wells criteria for DVT, and what score thresholds guide further workup?
Turn 2 — verification
List every specific criterion, point value, and score threshold you cited. Verify each independently and correct any errors. Flag any item you are uncertain about.
Use when
Drug factsDoses, contraindications, monitoring thresholds you might act on
Diagnostic criteriaAny scoring system, criteria set, or threshold value
Literature summariesTrial results, statistics, study conclusions
List-based answersAny response containing multiple specific claims
Key limitation: CoVe helps the model catch its own errors — but a first-year student may not recognize when the verification itself is wrong. This technique lowers hallucination risk; it does not replace independent verification of facts you will act on clinically.
Sahoo et al. (2024); original: Dhuliawala et al. (2023), arXiv:2309.11495
VS
Verbalized Sampling
Ask for options, not just one answer
For open-ended tasks — generate a distribution of responses to choose from
▶
Verbalized Sampling (VS) addresses a known problem: AI models trained with human feedback converge on a single "typical" response, suppressing the full range of plausible answers. VS works by explicitly asking the model to generate multiple distinct responses with a probability or confidence weight for each — recovering diversity from the model's underlying knowledge. Research shows VS improves output diversity 1.6–2.1× without sacrificing quality. For medical students, it is most useful when you need options to evaluate, not a single answer to accept.
How to use it
1
Ask for N distinct responses — "generate 4 distinct..." or "give me 5 different approaches to..."
2
Request a probability or weight — "with a relative probability for each" or "rank by how likely each is to be useful in this context"
3
Request diversity — optionally add "include less common or unconventional options, not just the most typical answer"
4
You choose — apply your own judgment to select or combine the best options
Examples
Prompt
Generate 5 distinct ways to open a conversation with a patient about starting insulin for the first time, each with a different framing or emphasis, and a relative probability that it would reduce patient anxiety. Include both common and less conventional approaches — not just the most typical explanation.
Prompt
Generate 6 distinct research questions examining the relationship between social determinants of health and asthma outcomes in urban pediatric populations. For each, provide a relative probability that it represents an understudied gap in the current literature. Include both conventional and more novel angles.
Prompt
Generate 5 distinct mnemonics or frameworks for remembering the causes of metabolic alkalosis, each using a different memory strategy (acronym, story, visual association, etc.), with a relative probability that each would be easy to recall under exam pressure. Include both well-known and less common approaches.
Use when
BrainstormingResearch questions, QI ideas, study frameworks where breadth matters
CommunicationFinding the right way to explain something to a patient or attending
DraftingGetting multiple versions of an abstract, case write-up, or presentation
Learning strategiesMultiple mnemonics, analogies, or explanations of a concept
Do not use VS when you need one correct factual answer — for example, the correct dose of a medication or a diagnostic threshold. Multiple options with probabilities is not helpful and potentially dangerous when there is a single right answer. Use CoVe instead.
Zhang et al. (2025), arXiv:2510.01171 — Verbalized Sampling

Evaluate yourself — three layers

Before you act on any AI output, run through this checklist. Check each item only when you are satisfied. All six must be checked before proceeding.

I gave sufficient context My prompt included the relevant clinical or research scenario with enough detail for the model to give a specific, applicable answer — not a generic one.
I selected the right technique I used CoT for reasoning, CoVe for fact-sensitive outputs, or VS for open-ended tasks — not the same technique by default for every prompt.
I specified a format I told the model how to structure the output — step-by-step list, table, bullet points, word limit — so the response is usable, not a wall of text.
I know enough to spot a wrong answer I have enough background knowledge on this topic to recognize if the output contains a significant error. If not, I will independently verify before acting on it.
I did not include PHI My prompt contains no patient names, MRNs, dates of service, or any identifying information. I described the scenario in general terms only.
I will verify any facts I act on Any specific doses, criteria, thresholds, or citations in this output will be cross-checked against a primary source before I use them clinically or academically.

Common mistakes — what students get wrong most often

These failure modes are specific to medical students and residents using AI tools. Each one has a fix.

Treating AI output as a source of truth
▶

The most common and dangerous mistake. A confident, well-structured AI response feels authoritative — but the model has no way of knowing what it doesn't know. It will state an incorrect drug dose with the same tone as a correct one. Medical students, still building their knowledge base, are particularly vulnerable because they may not yet have the background to recognize errors.

The fix: Treat every AI output as a smart first draft written by someone who might be wrong. Verify any fact you plan to act on — clinically, academically, or in a presentation — against an authoritative source. Use CoVe to at least make the model check itself first.
Vague prompts producing vague answers
▶

"Explain heart failure" produces a textbook overview. "Tell me about anticoagulation" produces a wall of generic content. The AI can only be as specific as the question. Students often start too broad because they're not sure what they need — and the vague output then feels like it confirmed they understood nothing.

The fix: Before prompting, write down the one specific thing you want to understand, decide, or produce. Then add: your clinical or research context, the format you want the output in, and any constraints (word count, reading level, specific guideline). Specific in, specific out.
Using the same technique for every task
▶

Once students discover "think step by step," they apply it to everything — including tasks where it produces unnecessary length without added value, and tasks where CoVe or VS would serve them far better. Technique selection is itself a skill.

The fix: Before prompting, ask yourself: Am I reasoning through something complex? (CoT) Am I getting facts I might act on? (CoVe) Do I need multiple options to evaluate? (VS) The "Which one?" tab in this guide walks through this decision for common student tasks.
Entering patient information into a public AI tool
▶

Pasting a clinic note, discharge summary, or any text containing a patient name, MRN, date of birth, or dates of service into ChatGPT, Claude, or any public AI tool is a HIPAA violation — regardless of whether you are a student or attending. This includes "anonymizing" by deleting the name but leaving other identifying details.

The fix: Describe clinical scenarios in the third person with no identifying information: "a 34-year-old with lupus nephritis" not the actual patient. Use institution-provided, HIPAA-compliant tools for any work that touches real patient data.
Stopping at the first response
▶

AI conversations are iterative. The first response is the starting point. Students who treat it as the final answer miss the most powerful part of these tools: the ability to push back, ask for clarification, request a different format, or apply CoVe as a follow-up to verify what was just said.

The fix: Think of AI interaction like a conversation with a knowledgeable colleague — one who needs to be challenged. Follow up: "Can you be more specific about...?" "I'm not sure I agree with step 3, walk me through that again." "Now verify the specific facts you just stated."
Using AI to skip understanding, not build it
▶

Asking AI to write your case presentation, generate your research question, or produce your study notes and then submitting them as your own work short-circuits the learning process. More importantly, it leaves you unable to defend or build on the output — which matters enormously in clinical settings where you will be asked to explain your reasoning.

The fix: Use AI to deepen understanding, not replace effort. Ask it to explain, challenge, or generate options — then engage with those options using your own judgment. The goal is to become the person who can evaluate AI output critically, not the person who depends on it uncritically.
